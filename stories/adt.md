# Stories: Algebraic Data Types

> **See also:** [`spec/adt.md`](../spec/adt.md) — the rules these chapters explain.

## Two constructs, against the hype

The first decision is one almost every modern language makes the other way, and we made it deliberately, knowing that. The mainstream — Rust, Swift, OCaml, Haskell — gives you a single construct that covers both payloadless constants and payload-carrying alternatives. `enum Color { Red, Green, Blue }` and `enum Expr { IntLit(String), Flip(Box<Expr>) }` are the *same keyword*, one general sum type that degenerates to a plain constant set when none of the cases happen to carry data. It is a clean, celebrated unification, and it is the road we did not take.

We did consider it — a single unified sum construct sat with us for a while — but it never actually cohered, and the more we looked the less sense it made. The two things a "sum type" is being asked to be are not one idea seen at two zoom levels; they are different ideas. A set of colors, weekdays, operators is a set of **interchangeable peers**: you iterate them, ordinal-index them, build a total table over them, ask "which one is this" and get a uniform answer for every case. A sum like `Expr` is the opposite — its cases are **heterogeneous**, each carrying its own payload, and the only thing they share is that a value is exactly one of them at a time. Fold both into one keyword and the peer operations — iteration, total mapping — get quietly mixed with cases where they are meaningless, because you cannot iterate the *values* of `IntLit(String)` the way you iterate `Red, Green, Blue`. The unification buys one keyword and pays for it by blurring the line between "uniform peers" and "tagged payloads," which is exactly the line we care about.

So Zane splits them. An [`enum`](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#2-enums) is a closed set of lowercase, payloadless peers that mean one uniform thing. A [`variant`](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#3-variants) is a sum type whose members each carry a payload. The property that decides which one you have is **uniformity** — the substitutability of intent — [not structure](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#21-why-an-enum-is-not-a-variant): the moment a case needs its own heterogeneous payload it is a `variant`, and as long as the members are interchangeable it is an `enum`. Keeping them apart lets each stay simple — enums uniform and tabulatable, variants heterogeneous and dispatched — instead of overloading one construct with both jobs.

The same strong stance shows up in what we refused to build on top: no interfaces, no constraints over enums. The temptation is to let two enums that keep appearing together share some abstract contract. But if two enums genuinely travel together all the time, that is evidence they are *one* enum and should be merged; and if they do not, wrapping them in a `variant` plus a few matching functions is a fair price for a rare case. Building open-extension machinery for a situation that resolves itself either way is overengineering, so there is none.

The cost is real and we are not going to pretend otherwise. Two keywords is more surface than one, and there is now a judgement call at the boundary — is this thing an `enum` or a `variant`? — that the unified design never forces you to make. Going against a near-universal convention also has a teaching cost: a programmer arriving from Rust reaches for one `enum` to do both jobs and has to be retrained. And splitting the constructs is what creates the loose end that [enum maps](#payloadless-peers-keep-their-data-outside) later have to tie off — because once enum members are payloadless on principle, per-member data has to attach from somewhere else, which is more machinery than just writing a field on a case. We think the clarity is worth all of it, but it is not free.

## One body, product or sum

Having split `variant` off from `enum`, the variant still needs a body — a way to write down its members and their types — and here the pull was the opposite of the last chapter. There, we resisted unifying two things; here, we leaned into a symmetry that was genuinely there. A `variant`'s body is a set of named, typed members. So is a `struct`'s. Rather than invent a second grammar for that, Zane makes them [byte-for-byte identical](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#31-the-structvariant-symmetry): `struct { a A; b B; }` and `variant { a A; b B; }` share one body shape, and the keyword is the only thing that changes.

The road not taken was a dedicated sum syntax — Rust's `Ctor(T)` cases with their own parenthesized payloads, OCaml's `| Ctor of t` bars. Those read fine, but they invent a distinct notation for what is structurally the same thing as a struct field: a name and a type. Reusing the struct body instead makes the and/or duality legible at a glance — same body, `struct` means *has `a` **and** `b`*, `variant` means *has `a` **or** `b`* — and the keyword flips four things in lockstep. Meaning flips from and to or. Construction flips from "set all members" to "set exactly one." Layout flips from the sum of the member sizes to a tag plus the max. And the read flips from total to partial: reading `e.a` on a struct always gives you an `A`, but reading `e.a` on a variant gives you an `A` only if `a` is the live case.

That partial read is the one consequence worth dwelling on, because it could have gone several ways. A case that is not live has no value to hand back, and the usual answers are a sentinel (null, a zero value) or a silent failure. Zane makes the read [abortable](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#3-variants) instead — a bifurcated return that flows through the ordinary `?`/`??` error path — so a partial read is honest about being partial and cannot be mistaken for a total one. A single-payload case, once bound, behaves as its payload, so you reach through it directly; but you only get there by acknowledging the read might not fire. The primary consumer is never meant to be the ad-hoc member read anyway — it is exhaustive dispatch, the subject of the next two chapters.

Orthogonal to all of this is the value/reference axis, and it is what decides recursion. A plain `variant` is a value sum — copied on assignment, transitively a value, and therefore non-recursive; a `#variant` is a reference sum with identity that may hold `&` payloads and may [recurse](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#4-recursion-and-storage). This is not an ADT rule so much as the whole language's [identity-is-opt-in](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/foundations.md#7-identity-is-opt-in) commitment showing up here: a value type cannot hold the `&` that a recursive case must box through, so a recursive sum like `Expr` has to be a `#variant`, never a value `variant`. The indirection is always an explicit `&` — no hidden auto-boxing, the way `Box` is hidden in some languages — because ownership and references are explicit everywhere in Zane. The deeper argument for why a value type is closed inline storage that cannot recurse belongs to the [memory story](../stories/memory.md); here it is enough that the same body grammar serves all four kinds and the `#` modifier is what decides which may recurse (see [`memory.md` §2.10](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/memory.md#210-value-downstream-enforcement-transitive-value-only-field-restriction) for the transitive-value rule).

The cost of the shared body is small but worth naming: you cannot tell a product from a sum by looking at the body, only by reading the keyword in front of it, and a variant can never grow a surface that deliberately reads *differently* from a struct even where that might aid a reader. We think making the duality visible is worth giving up that freedom, and in practice the keyword is right there. It is a far cheaper cost than the one the next chapter takes on.

## Escaping the matcher machine

With variants defined and their reads made honest, the real question is how you *consume* one — and this is where Zane departs hardest from the ML tradition, out of a concrete irritation rather than a theory. The irritation is scale. Textbook pattern matching is always shown on a toy: three or four cases, a one-line body each, the whole thing fitting in a tidy block. The sum types we actually care about are not like that. An AST `Expr` has dozens of cases, and the logic for each is not a one-liner — it is real work, sometimes a lot of it.

Pour that into the ML answer — a single `match`/`case` expression with the scrutinee on top and every arm below — and two things go wrong at once. First, it becomes a **matcher machine**: one enormous expression that every case's logic is crammed inside, the function equivalent of a thousand-line switch. Second, and this is the part that actually decided the design, the structure *forces depth*. The `match` is one level of nesting; each arm's body is a second; so the substantial per-case work starts two levels deep and only goes deeper from there. You pay indentation on precisely the code that matters most — the handler bodies — in every single arm, permanently. For a handful of arms that is invisible. For thirty arms of real logic it is a tax you notice every day.

The move that gets out of this came first, before any inline match syntax existed, and it is the heart of the whole design: **bind the arm to the function itself.** Instead of arms nested inside a match expression, each case-handler becomes an ordinary top-level function [overloaded on the case](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#5-pattern-matching-via-case-overload-dispatch):

```zane
String exprToString(x Expr.intLit)   => toString(x)
String exprToString(x Expr.floatLit) => ...
```

The dispatch is *unwrapped* — spread flat across named definitions rather than stacked inside one expression. Every handler body sits back at its natural, single level of depth. There is no matcher machine, because there is no one place the cases pile up; there is just a family of ordinary functions that happen to share a name and partition a variant between them. What would have been arm bodies two levels deep are now function bodies one level deep, and each can grow as large as it needs to without dragging the others down with it.

The compiler makes this safe by treating all overloads of one function name over a variant's cases as a single **match set** and checking that the set covers every case — an incomplete set is a compile-time error. It lowers to a runtime tag jump, distinct from ordinary static overload resolution (see [`functions.md` §4.4](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/functions.md#44-variant-case-overloads)): a value whose static type is the whole variant becomes an O(1) jump over the case overloads, while a value already narrowed to a case picks its arm statically with no jump at all. Two rules fall out of doing dispatch this way. Exhaustiveness is checked **per package**, not per variant — the arms of one match must be co-located so a single compilation unit can verify completeness — which is what lets the type stay *closed* while operations stay *open*: any package may import a variant and declare its own complete match over the cases, because the cases are fixed and all visible, but no one may *split* one match across packages. And a whole-variant overload `f(x V)` cannot coexist with case overloads `f(x V.case)` for the same name, because a call would be ambiguous between matching `V` directly and unwrapping it; forbidding the coexistence removes the ambiguity by construction.

The cost is the mirror image of the benefit, and it is a genuine one. Unwrapping the dispatch into separate functions **destroys locality.** You can no longer see all thirty arms in one place; they are scattered across the package, and the *shape* of the dispatch — the fact that these functions together partition `Expr` — is implicit in the set of overloads rather than visible as a single block. For a reader whose question is "what are all the cases and how is each handled," the very matcher machine we were fleeing is actually the better artifact, because it is one block you can read top to bottom. That tension — flat but scattered, versus nested but co-located — is real, and it is exactly what pushed us to add an inline form as well, which is where the next chapter starts. There is also plain extra machinery here: the per-package exhaustiveness rule and the mutual-exclusion rule are two things a reader has to hold that a simple local match would never need.

## Variants, not patterns

The scattering that case overloads cause is what made us want a second, local form — something for the times when a handful of arms genuinely belong together in one place and you would rather see them as a block than hunt for them across a package. That is the [`match` expression](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#6-the-match-expression): a scrutinee and a flat `[ ]` list of callables, dispatching on the tag to the callable whose parameter type matches the live case. It came second, after case overloads, and it is deliberately the small-scale tool, not the thing you reach for at AST scale.

Its defining choice is that it has **no special arm syntax** — the arms are ordinary function values, lambda literals or named lambda-variables, so an arm can be named, reused, and passed like any other callable. That is very much in the grain of the rest of the language, which keeps reaching for existing machinery instead of minting new syntax. But we will be honest that we are not sure it is right. The `match` expression may conform to that generality *too* hard: insisting an arm is just a function value whose parameter type happens to be a case buys consistency, but the surface it produces — a bracketed list of lambdas — is arguably more ceremony than the common case deserves, and a dedicated arm might simply read better. We have not settled this, and the opinion may move; we are recording it here as a live tension rather than dressing it up as a finished win. It is the one part of this document we would least defend to the death.

Underneath both dispatch forms sits the decision the whole area actually rests on, and it is a decision about scope, not mechanism. The ML and Rust world calls this feature **pattern matching**, and that name smuggles in a whole kit: nested patterns that reach several layers down in one arm, or-patterns, guards, as-bindings, structural destructuring of tuples and records and literals. Zane takes none of it, on purpose. Our disagreement is with the framing itself — "pattern matching" runs together two separable things: *dispatching on a sum's tag*, and *destructuring arbitrary shape*. We think the tag-dispatch is the part that carries the weight, and the "pattern" half is not that important. We care a great deal about variants; we care very little about patterns. So Zane matches variants — a [one-level tag unwrap](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#5-pattern-matching-via-case-overload-dispatch), a single-payload case behaving as its payload once bound — and stops there. No nested dispatch, no guards, no shape-binding.

Refusing full structural patterns rests on two arguments, and the second is the one that really convinced us. The first is the familiar one: a pattern language is a *second language* — its own grammar, its own binding rules, its own nesting — grafted onto the host, and Zane spends its whole budget avoiding exactly that kind of bespoke sublanguage. But the sharper argument is that **deep patterns reintroduce the very nesting we just spent a chapter escaping.** A nested pattern is depth *in the matcher*; the entire move of the last chapter was to flatten dispatch *outward* into functions rather than push it *inward* into richer arms. Those two instincts point in opposite directions, and once we saw that, the choice made itself: Zane flattens. Adding nested patterns would be undoing the thing that makes the design ours.

We want to state the OCaml comparison without flinching, because it cuts both ways. Measured on structural pattern matching as such, Zane is *deliberately weaker* than OCaml — it cannot destructure three levels deep in a single arm, and when you genuinely need that you nest matches, or reach through abortable member access, by hand. That is a real expressiveness cost and we are not going to hide it behind the "it's a non-goal" phrasing. But measured on the thing sum types are usually *for* — dispatching over a large closed set of cases, each with real work — case overloads are, we would argue, better than a thirty-arm match, because they do not pay the nesting tax. The bet the whole design makes is that the second workload is the common and important one for the data we care about, and the first is rare enough to do by hand when it comes up. Whether that bet holds across everything people build is the open question this chapter leaves on the table.

The costs, gathered in one place: deep destructuring is manual and occasionally clumsy; the `match` expression's generality is possibly overbuilt for its job; and standing against the "pattern matching" grain has a teaching cost every bit as real as the one in the first chapter — newcomers arrive expecting guards and nested patterns and meet a smaller, sharper tool instead, and some of them will read the smallness as a gap rather than a stance. The abort integration that lets a `match` pass an abortable arm's failure one level up is the one place this all reconnects to the wider language (see [`error-handling.md` §3.5](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/error-handling.md#35-match-is-abort-transparent)).

## Payloadless peers keep their data outside

The first chapter left a loose end dangling, and this is where it gets tied off. Splitting `enum` from `variant` and keeping enum members strictly payloadless raises an obvious question: where does per-member data go? A weekday has a display name, a color has an RGB triple, an operator has a precedence. In the unified world you would just write the datum as a payload on the case. Zane forbids that by construction — the moment a member carries data it is no longer an interchangeable peer — so the data has to attach from outside the enum entirely.

The answer is the [enum map](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/35b19cfca21c6cb1609ec0b8000c293a468b156e/spec/adt.md#7-enum-maps): a package-scope, exhaustive, access-only declaration that pairs each member with a value and is read field-style.

```zane
Colors.colorName String [ red = "Red", green = "Green", blue = "Blue" ]

Colors.red.colorName   // "Red"
```

It names the property at the site where it is read, it reserves no keyword — deliberately, so `Map` and `Dict` stay free for a future dictionary type — and it is checked exhaustive exactly the way a match set is, so a forgotten member is a compile error rather than a hole. The reason it is a *map* and not a payload comes straight back to the first chapter's criterion: intrinsic data would make the member a variant case, whereas external data keeps the members payloadless and the *data* uniform, which is precisely the enum's defining property preserved rather than broken. A variant would never want an enum map, because a variant's data is intrinsic to each case by definition; the map exists specifically because an enum's does not.

One further line we drew: an enum map is not a passable value. The mapping is static — there is nothing to dispatch over, only a result to read — so making it a first-class value would be blessing something with runtime identity that has no runtime behavior. A genuinely *dynamic* `Colors → String` transform already has a name; it is a lambda, `String[Colors]`. Keeping the static table and the dynamic transform as different things means the map stays the simple static lookup it is, and anyone who needs real dispatch reaches for the function type instead of stretching the map to cover a job it was not built for.

The cost is the one the first chapter warned was coming. Per-member data now lives *away* from the member — you declare `Colors` in one place and its `colorName` table in another, and adding a member means touching every map that ranges over the enum (the exhaustiveness check will at least tell you which). For data that is genuinely intrinsic and always present, that separation can feel like bureaucracy next to just writing a field on the case. But that friction is also a signal worth listening to: if the data really is intrinsic to each member, you probably did not want an enum at all — you wanted a variant, and the map's awkwardness is the design telling you so.

## Variants deserve a central place

Everything to this point describes the dispatch we actually committed to — and then we talked ourselves out of its most distinctive piece. The case overloads of [Escaping the matcher machine](#escaping-the-matcher-machine) were built to kill the nesting tax, and they do kill it, but the longer we sat with the picture the more one cost swallowed the win. Spreading a variant's handling across a family of top-level functions buys flatness by spending **locality**: there is no longer any single place where the whole variant is seen being dealt with. And for a variant — of all types — that is exactly the wrong thing to give up. A sum's entire meaning is "one of these N things," and the thing you most want in front of you is all N handled together, as one exhaustive picture. Scatter that across N definitions and you have hidden the one structure that was worth showing. The dispatch table is not boilerplate to be dissolved; it is the artifact. **Variants deserve a central place.**

That sentence is what kickstarted the redesign, and the first thing it forced us to admit is that we had misdiagnosed the original problem. The nesting tax was real — a thirty-arm `match` with fat bodies does bury its important code two levels deep — but the fault was never the *block*, it was the *fat arms*. Keep the block and keep the arms thin, each one a shallow dispatch that hands off to a flat top-level function, and the tax simply is not paid: the block holds the dispatch, which is what you want central, while the work lives in ordinary functions the arms call. We had thrown out the central place to solve a problem that only ever needed the arms to lose weight. So the case overloads were the detour, and [the small `match` expression](#variants-not-patterns) we had kept as a secondary form was the right primary shape all along.

Making the block central let us delete the machinery it had been carrying precisely in order *not* to be central. The old arms were function values selected by their parameter type — an arm was a lambda whose parameter happened to name a case — and that contortion existed only to keep arms first-class and reusable. A central block does not want reusable arms; it wants the cases laid out in one place. So the parameter-type dispatch went, and the per-arm return-type ceremony went with it: the block declares its one result type once, at the front, not on every arm. What a block wants instead is for an arm to read as what it already is in Zane — [a **declaration**](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/cba59e81456a5ab65837f8e0124875886bb42834/spec/adt.md#51-the-block-and-its-arms). `x strLit => body` binds `x`, whose type is the case `strLit`, with the `Expr.` supplied by the scrutinee, exactly the way `x Int` binds a value elsewhere; the casing rule carries the whole thing with no new syntax. The binder is optional — a bare `strLit => body` when the body wants no payload — because an unbound head introduces no parse ambiguity and Zane already leaves names optional in the same spirit, most visibly in the `?` error handler that binds `? err { }` or does not.

Grouping several cases into one arm is where the surface paid us back for moving to a block. With the arms living in `{ }`, the `[ ]` that the old `match` had spent on its arm list was free again, and a bracketed set of cases is exactly what it should be spent on: `x [intLit, floatLit] => body`. This is the same bracket that *declares* a set of peers in `enum [ red, green, blue ]`, now *selecting* a set of cases — grouping cases in a match rhymes with grouping members in an enum, which is the kind of internal echo we trust. A singleton is then just the one-element case of a group, and the bracket stays a case *selector*, never a type: for a single case the binder is its payload as before, and for a group — whose payloads differ — the binder widens to the variant, so no anonymous unnamed type is ever conjured and the "every type must be named" rule stays whole.

The last thing the central place forced was the [removal of the default arm](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/cba59e81456a5ab65837f8e0124875886bb42834/spec/adt.md#52-exhaustive-with-no-default-arm), and this is the part we feel most strongly about. A catch-all carries no type information; all it says is "every case not matched above," and that set is **implicit and unstable** — it silently changes when the variant grows a case or when another arm is edited. So a new case added to `Expr` would vanish into the default without a word, which is precisely the accident exhaustiveness exists to prevent. A central place is only worth having if it is *complete*, and a default is an escape hatch out of completeness. So there is none: every case is named, singly or inside a `[ ]` group, and adding a case to the variant becomes a compile error at every match until someone decides where it goes. That is the whole point restated as a rule — the variant's central place must account for the variant, all of it, out loud.

None of this is free, and the bill lands in the same spot the default was covering. "Everything else" now has to be enumerated: a thirty-case variant where you truly handle two specially and the rest identically makes you list the twenty-eight, by name, in a bracket. That is more to type, and it is deliberately so — the list is a checked record, and the compiler keeps it honest as the variant changes — but it is real friction, and it is loudest where several scrutinees are matched together and a "regardless of state" arm has to spell out every state. The other cost is the one we opened with, now paid from the other side: choosing a central block over scattered overloads trades the flat top-level-function *style* for locality. Open operations survive — any package can still write its own `f(e Expr) => match e { ... }` over an imported variant — so nothing is truly lost there, but the particular pleasure of a variant's operation being a loose family of same-named functions is gone, on purpose, in exchange for being able to read the whole thing in one place. We think that is the right trade for a sum type. And it is still, unchanged, [variants and not patterns](#variants-not-patterns): [the block dispatches on the tag and nothing deeper](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/cba59e81456a5ab65837f8e0124875886bb42834/spec/adt.md#53-variant-matching-not-pattern-matching), and bracket-grouping is a set of tags, never a step toward matching shape.

One extension came later, and from a deliberate stock-take: after weighing where this design still lagged OCaml, we wanted to know which absences were principle and which were merely missing. Most of the elimination gap — nested patterns, guards — we were content to keep as principle. But one was not a matter of taste. OCaml matches a *pair* of values in one go, and a great many real dispatches — state machines above all — are joint, keyed on two tags at once rather than one. So we added [multi-scrutinee matching](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/82bb812662871827b48ac5b7089a409896636cbf/spec/adt.md#56-matching-several-scrutinees): a `match` takes a bare comma-separated list of scrutinees, and each arm gives one selector per position. The tell that this stays on the right side of the [variants-not-patterns](#variants-not-patterns) line is, once again, a missing pair of parentheses. `match state, event` is *several* variants matched together; wrap it as `(state, event)` and you have named a tuple to take apart, which is the pattern-matching road. Bare, it is only ever tag dispatch — now across a product of tags rather than one, the `and` to the arm-group's `or`. The cost is the one the no-default rule already charges, paid again on the product axis: exhaustiveness becomes the cross-product of the case sets, so a "whatever the state, on `reset`" arm has to name every state, and the table grows with each variant it joins. We judged that verbosity a fair price for finally being able to write the one shape the single-scrutinee block genuinely could not — the joint transition table.

## The group is sugar, not a widening

The central block shipped with one detail we got wrong on the first pass, and it hid inside the very sentence the [last chapter](#variants-deserve-a-central-place) ends on: bracket-grouping is a set of tags, never a type. We believed that and then wrote a binder rule that quietly broke it. A single-case arm `x strLit` binds `x` at that case's payload — obvious. But a *group* arm `x [intLit, floatLit]` groups cases whose payloads may differ, and we had to say what `x` then is. Our first answer was that it widens to the whole variant: give the binder the one type that covers every case in the bracket and the arm still type-checks. It looked conservative and safe.

It is neither, and we did not catch it ourselves — it surfaced in a conversation where someone repeated *their* model back to us, assuming a group was simply several arms sharing a body, and we realised their assumption was better than our spec. The trouble with widening is not that it is wasteful, though it is. It is that it *un-does the match*. The arm's whole reason to exist is that the tag jump has just proven `x` is one of `{intLit, floatLit}` — that proof is the thing the block is *for*. Widening then re-types `x` as the entire variant, which is strictly *less* than what was just established, so to actually use `x` you must abort-read it back into a case: re-running, by hand, the dispatch the jump already performed. You narrow, then un-narrow in the same breath. A construct whose one job is to pin down a tag cannot be allowed to throw that tag away as it binds it.

Stated that plainly, the fix is forced, and there were only ever three ways to type the binder. Keep widening — incoherent, for the reason just given. Narrow the binder to a sub-variant `{intLit, floatLit}` — this keeps the tag, but it conjures a type nobody named, and ["the bracket is a selector, not a type"](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/b856ad02bfcddfcbc0a47fb17f58edbd38c18fc5/spec/adt.md#51-the-block-and-its-arms) was a rule we had just spent a whole chapter defending; we were not going to break it one section later to patch a binder. Or the third road: stop treating the group as a thing that *has* a type at all, and treat it as pure syntax. [`x [intLit, floatLit] => body`](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/b856ad02bfcddfcbc0a47fb17f58edbd38c18fc5/spec/adt.md#51-the-block-and-its-arms) is nothing but `x intLit => body; x floatLit => body;` written once — two arms, each binding `x` at its own payload, each checked on its own. That was the model the conversation handed us, and it is the one that keeps every earlier promise at once: the tag is preserved, because each stamped arm carries its exact case; no unnamed type appears, because each binder is an already-named payload; and the bracket is a *purer* selector than widening ever made it, because now it selects nothing but the set of arms to stamp out.

What settled it was how much *disappears* when the group is only sugar. The binder-type rule we had been agonising over does not get rewritten — it gets deleted, because there is no such thing as a group binder, only ordinary single-case binders in arms that were written together. Exhaustiveness needs no special clause: N stamped arms cover N tags. The one-result-type rule already covers them. Even the multi-scrutinee grouping we had worried about separately falls straight out — [`[a, b], [c, d]`](https://github.com/zane-lang/spec/blob/b856ad02bfcddfcbc0a47fb17f58edbd38c18fc5/spec/adt.md#56-matching-several-scrutinees) is just the cross-product of stampings, which is exactly the cross-product exhaustiveness the joint block already demanded. A rule that dissolves three special cases into one desugaring is almost always the right rule; we should have reached for it before inventing a type for a bracket that never wanted one.

The cost is real and it lands in one place: the binder can no longer stand for the whole variant, ever. Under widening you could write `x [a, b] => useVariant(x)` and hand the whole value off; now `x` is a payload in each stamped arm, and that call does not type-check unless `useVariant` is overloaded across the payloads. The answer is that the scrutinee is still in scope — an arm that wants the variant reads the scrutinee, not the binder — which is clean except where the scrutinee is an unnamed temporary and there is nothing to refer to, a narrow corner we are recording as a known edge rather than papering over. The deeper cost is set by the [no-interface stance](#two-constructs-against-the-hype): because Zane has no contract spanning arbitrary types, the only thing you can *do* with a binder shared across differing payloads is call something overloaded for each, so a shared binder pays off exactly where such an overload family already exists and does nothing for a genuinely heterogeneous "everything else" group — which is why those groups are, in practice, left unbound. That is the honest shape of it: the sugar is free and the per-case checking it implies is free, and the one thing it cannot do is manufacture a common interface the language has deliberately chosen not to have. It remains, throughout, [variants and not patterns](#variants-not-patterns) — the stamped arms are more tag arms, never a step toward matching shape.
